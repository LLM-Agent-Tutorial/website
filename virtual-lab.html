The Virtual Lab: AI–human Collaboration in Medical Research

Paper ↗ GitHub ↗

The Virtual Lab facilitates interdisciplinary research through collaboration between AI agents and human researchers. Initially the human researcher defines two primary agents—a Principal Investigator (PI) and a Scientific Critic. The PI assembles a specialized team and orchestrates both team and individual meetings to design a comprehensive workflow for the research topic, and the human researcher ultimately executes the finalized scripts to complete the project.

Up next, we outline how the Virtual Lab’s role-played PI and Scientific Critic apply reflection, coordinated team meetings, and tool generation workflows to drive medical research.

Role-play

The Virtual Lab introduces two key roles: a Principal Investigator (PI) agent acting as the primary coordinator, and a Scientific Critic agent that provides critical oversight and identifies potential risks. Together, they ensure high-quality decision-making and maintain workflow transparency.

Example of role-playing as a principal investigator

Title: Principal Investigator

Expertise: Running a science research lab.

Goal: Perform research in your area of expertise that maximizes the scientific impact of the work.

Role: Lead a team of experts to solve an important scientific problem, make key decisions based on team input, and manage the project timeline and resources.

Reflection

Once the human researcher defines a high-level goal, the PI determines which specialist agents are needed in an individual team generation meeting. The Scientific Critic provides targeted feedback after each response, guiding agents toward improved outcomes.

Example of team generation (Principal Investigator)

INPUT: Project: develop antibodies or nanobodies for the newest SARS-CoV-2 variant. Task: Generate a team of 3 scientists.

OUTPUT:

Agent 1 — Title: Computational Biologist · Expertise: Protein structure prediction and modeling · Goal: Develop and refine predictive models for protein-antibody interactions · Role: Provide insights into molecular dynamics and structural constraints impacting antibody design.

Agent 2: ...

Example of reflection (Scientific Critic)

INPUT: Chat History: ... · Task: Please critique (agent)'s most recent answer.

OUTPUT: As the Scientific Critic, I would like to address ...

Cooperation

Team meetings involve collaborative discussions among the PI, scientist agents, and the Scientific Critic. The human researcher sets an agenda, the PI initiates the conversation, agents provide insights in turn, and the PI synthesizes input, summarizes decisions, and identifies next steps.

Example of team meeting start (Principal Investigator)

INPUT: Agenda questions supplied by the human researcher (e.g., Will you design standard antibodies or nanobodies?). Team members include the Computational Biologist, Scientific Critic, ...

OUTPUT: "Thank you, everyone, for joining this important meeting. Our task is to ..."

Example of team member discussion (Computational Biologist)

INPUT: Chat History: ... · Task: Provide your thoughts on the discussion (round 2 of 5).

OUTPUT: "As the Computational Biologist, I will focus on ..."

Example of summarizing the discussion (Principal Investigator)

INPUT: Chat History: ... · Task: Summarize the meeting, provide recommendations, and answer agenda questions.

OUTPUT: Summarization of each team member ... Recommendation: focus on ... Answers: "1. Will you design standard antibodies or nanobodies? Answer: Nanobodies. Justification: Nanobodies offer superior stability..." Next Steps: Data acquisition, model development, ...

Tool use

After finalizing project details, agents move through tool generation and tool implementation meetings with human assistance. Teams brainstorm candidate tools (AlphaFold, Rosetta, etc.), then the PI assigns tools and specialist agents generate structured scripts that humans later execute.

Example of tool generation discussion (Computational Biologist)

INPUT: Project context and summaries of previous meetings. Task: List several tools (5–10) that would be relevant.

OUTPUT: "Apply AlphaFold or Rosetta to model the 3D structures of the top-ranked nanobody variants..."

Example of tool implementation (Computational Biologist)

INPUT: Decision: modify existing nanobodies. Task provided by the human researcher: use Rosetta to calculate binding energy in three parts.

OUTPUT: Part 1 — RosettaScripts XML ... · Part 2 — Rosetta command example · Part 3 — Python script to process score files.
